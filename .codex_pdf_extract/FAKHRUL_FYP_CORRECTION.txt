ABSTRACT 
 
 
In today’s digital age, many students spend most of their time using mobile devices, 
which makes traditional Islamic education often based on textbooks and lectures less 
effective and less appealing. As a result, young Muslims, new converts (mu’allaf), 
and non-Muslims interested in Islam may struggle to stay engaged or understand key 
teachings. To solve this problem, F.I.K.H. (Fun Islamic Knowledge Hub) was 
developed as a mobile application that applies game-based learning to make learning 
Islamic knowledge more interesting, interactive, and enjoyable. This project focuses 
on three main areas of Islamic learning which are Aqidah (beliefs), Fiqh (practices), 
and Akhlak (ethics), with added content from Hadith to support deeper 
understanding. The app was designed using the Game Development Life Cycle 
(GDLC) method and includes three games, Laluan Hijrah (Snakes & Ladders), Kotak 
Fathonah (Bingo), and Padanan Fikrah (Memory Match), each linked to one of the 
core learning areas. It also features flashcards with 20 subtopics per subject and 50 
authentic Hadiths from sources like Bukhari and Muslim. To encourage regular 
learning, the app uses gamification elements such as streak bars, leaderboards, daily 
tasks, badges, and scores. The application was developed using Android Studio, 
Firebase, and Figma, and went through usability testing with 20 respondents. Results 
showed that learners were more motivated and found the learning experience more 
engaging compared to traditional study methods. In conclusion, F.I.K.H. shows how 
Islamic education can evolve by using technology to better connect with modern 
learners. By combining core religious knowledge with game-based learning and 
interactive tools, the app helps users not only learn but also enjoy and apply Islamic 
teachings in their daily lives.

1.1 ​ Background of Project 
 
F.I.K.H. (Fun Islamic Knowledge Hub) is a game-based learning application 
focused on teaching Aqidah, Fiqh, and Akhlak to learners of all ages. 
Traditionally, Islamic education has been delivered through direct instruction 
from an ustaz, teacher, or mentor. While effective, this method may not 
always be comfortable or accessible for every individual. For instance, some 
learners, especially older Muslims might feel hesitant to seek help due to fear 
of embarrassment or judgment. F.I.K.H. fills this gap by offering a 
welcoming, learner-centered environment where users can explore Islamic 
teachings at their own pace, regardless of age, background, or prior 
knowledge. 
 
In today’s era of mobile technology and digital interactivity, engaging 
platforms are increasingly essential. Game-based learning (GBL) lies at the 
heart of F.I.K.H.'s approach, enabling users to interact with content in a way 
that promotes active participation and sustained interest. As noted by Greipl 
et al. (2020), GBL enhances learning by integrating rewards, challenges, and 
a sense of progress, all of which contribute to a more effective educational 
experience. Similarly, Khadijah et al. (2022) emphasize that GBL elevates 
1 
 
both teaching and learning to new levels of interactivity and enjoyment. By 
embedding Islamic education within a game-based framework, F.I.K.H. seeks 
to make learning not only informative but also meaningful and motivating. 
 
1.2 ​ Problem Statement 
 
Learning Islam should be an enlightening, clear, and accessible experience for 
everyone. Ideally, Islamic studies such as Aqidah, Fiqh, and Akhlak should 
not confuse or overwhelm learners. The learning journey should feel inviting 
and simple, allowing individuals to explore their faith with confidence and 
clarity. In today’s era of mobile technology and fast-paced lifestyles, digital 
tools have become essential in making learning more efficient and adaptable. 
They provide flexibility, access to resources, and opportunities for interactive 
learning. With the right design, digital platforms can support learners in a 
personalized and meaningful way. Game-based learning (GBL) is one such 
modern method that has proven to be especially effective. According to 
Pratama and Azhari (2020), gamified learning encourages factual memory 
and sustained engagement, making it an optimal method for long-term 
understanding. Rather than relying solely on traditional teaching, digital 
solutions can complement religious education with modern strategies that 
increase motivation. When combined with authentic content, digital tools can 
turn the study of Islam into a more enjoyable and lasting experience. The 
potential is high, what is needed is the right balance between credible Islamic 
knowledge and engaging learning formats that meet the needs of today’s 
learners. 
 
Despite the availability of learning resources, the reality is that many people 
still struggle when trying to learn Islam especially new Muslims (muallaf) or 
non-Muslims who are simply curious. For them, the journey can be confusing 
and isolating. They often do not know where to start, what to read, or who to 
ask. This hesitation may be worsened by the fear of being judged or 
misunderstood. Older Muslims who are trying to return to the path of learning 
often share this fear as well. Islamic knowledge spans many areas, and the 
sheer amount of information can feel overwhelming to anyone starting from 
2 
 
scratch. Moreover, current digital tools often lack either the depth or 
authenticity needed to truly help. As Memon et al. (2020) observed, the lack 
of engaging tools in religious learning environments places the burden 
entirely on teachers, many of whom rely on outdated methods. Mohamad 
Yusof and Zulkifli (2021) also pointed out that some Islamic educators 
themselves are untrained or unprepared to use effective strategies, especially 
for teaching foundational subjects like Jawi. Meanwhile, Khadijah H. et al.

innovative, digital-based solutions that could make learning more accessible 
and engaging. This leads towards the creation of such classroom situations 
where students are seen as mere recipients of information, instead of 
inquisitive and active participants of their own educational experience. 
 
As a result of these challenges, the experience of learning Islam can often feel 
dry, repetitive, or even discouraging. Without the right support and 
engagement, learners may find it hard to connect with the content in a 
meaningful way. Even if they manage to absorb the information, it often feels 
disconnected from their daily lives, making it difficult to apply. This lack of

uninterested or disconnected, their spiritual growth may be delayed, and 
worse, they might lose the desire to learn more about their faith altogether.

1.4 ​ Scope 
 
F.I.K.H. application is a mobile application designed to deliver Islamic 
knowledge in an interactive, engaging, and welcoming way. Built for 
Android, this fully developed platform integrates game-based learning (GBL) 
and implements gamification strictly as supporting game elements to make 
the process of learning Islam more accessible and enjoyable for all users. This 
is consistent with the findings of Abdullah and Abdul Razak (2021), who 
identified that gamified education raises the interest and acceptance of 
religious study among the learners. The app focuses on four key areas of 
Islamic Learning which are Fiqh (jurisprudence), Aqidah (creed), Akhlak 
(morality), and Hadith (Prophetic traditions). These are introduced through 
three educational games such as Laluan Hijrah, Kotak Fathonah, and Padanan 
Fikrah, each tied to one of the main topics. Every game includes 50 
multiple-choice quiz questions, adding up to 150 questions in total, with 
instant feedback to aid learning and improve retention. 
 
Beyond gameplay, F.I.K.H. features a flashcard module containing 20 
subtopics each for Fiqh, Aqidah, and Akhlak, offering concise definitions and 
explanations. The Hadith section contains 50 authentic hadiths sourced from 
reputable collections such as Bukhari, Muslim, and Tirmidhi, helping users 
connect with the teachings of the Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) in a clear and 
simplified way. To enhance motivation, the app uses game elements such as 
leaderboards, score systems, badges, streak bars, progress bars, titles, and 
daily challenges. These are designed to promote consistency, celebrate 
achievement, and maintain user interest over time. The application is intended 
for everyone, regardless of age, religious background, or familiarity with 
Islamic teachings, welcoming young learners, adults, reverts (muallaf), and 
even non-Muslims. 
 
Developed using the Game Development Life Cycle (GDLC), the app went 
through key phases such as requirement analysis, game design, development, 
and testing. While the main interface is in English, Malay is used for

localized terms like game titles and badge names. The scope of this 
application is limited to online learning and does not include advanced 
Islamic topics, multiplayer modes, or offline access. Instead, it focuses on 
delivering basic, authentic content suitable for beginners and intermediate 
learners, and can also be used as a self-study tool or classroom supplement.

2.1 ​ Overview of Islamic Learning 
 
Islam is a monotheistic faith revealed by Allah (God) to Prophet Muhammad 
(peace be upon him) in the 7th century CE on the Arabian Peninsula. The 
word Islam means submission to the will of Allah, promoting peace, moral 
virtue, and devotion to divine guidance. Muslims believe that the Prophet 
Muhammad (PBUH) is the final messenger in a line of prophets, regarded as 
the ideal model of leadership, compassion, and integrity (Santoso, 2024). The 
two main sources of Islamic knowledge are the Qur’an, the holy book, and 
the Sunnah, which includes the sayings, actions, and approvals of the Prophet. 
The Sunnah plays a vital role in understanding the Quran and applying its 
teachings in daily life (Ashimi, 2023). 
 
Islamic learning is not restricted to a formal period but is a continuous 
journey of developing knowledge, behavior, and spirituality. Islamic Studies, 
as an academic field, explores Islam through branches like Tafsir, Hadith, 
Fiqh, Aqidah, Akhlak, Sirah, Usul al-Fiqh, Islamic history, Tasawwuf, and 
Arabic. Hadith provides moral and legal guidance next to the Qur’an 
(Ashimi, 2023). Akhlak focuses on personal ethics, promoting values such as 
humility, patience, and responsibility (Ashimi, 2023). Sirah, the Prophet’s 
biography, offers a historical and ethical model of exemplary living (Santoso, 
2024). These fields complement each other, shaping not only intellect but also 
spiritual character (Safariani et al., 2023). Ultimately, the goal of Islamic 
education is not just knowledge, but the formation of morally upright 
individuals guided by divine values (Santoso, 2024). 
 
While Islamic Studies often takes place in academic or religious institutions, 
Islamic learning can happen anywhere. In the digital age, mobile applications 
offer an opportunity to reach broader audiences especially muallaf, youth, and 
non-Muslims exploring Islam. The F.I.K.H. application focuses on three 
essential topics such as Fiqh, Aqidah, and Akhlak, which together form the 
foundation of Islamic belief, practice, and moral character. The inclusion of 
Hadith serves strictly as an additional topic to reinforce the core teachings 
10 
 
and provide practical context through the sayings and actions of the Prophet 
Muhammad (PBUH). By integrating interactive, game-based learning, 
F.I.K.H. serves as a bridge between traditional Islamic knowledge and 
modern learning methods, enabling users to understand, internalize, and live 
by Islamic teachings in a meaningful and relevant way. 
 
2.1.1 ​Akhlak  
 
Akhlak in the Islamic learning system is the moral and ethical code that 
guides a Muslim’s conduct toward Allah, oneself, and others. It goes beyond 
outward etiquette, reflecting the condition of the heart and soul, and plays an 
essential role in shaping individual behavior and fostering a harmonious 
society (Nabi U. et al., 2024). It is expressed through dimensions such as 
worship, sincerity, self-discipline, kindness, and respect. Ruzairi and Jasmi 
(2022) highlight aspects like goodness, unity, morality, politeness, and 
humanity as core components of Islamic manners that help build character 
and influence social interaction. Akhlak also promotes positive traits such as 
honesty, patience, compassion, humility, and warns against negative values 
like arrogance, envy, and dishonesty. In Islam, Prophet Muhammad (peace be 
upon him) is regarded as uswatun hasanah (an excellent example), embodying 
the highest standard of character in all aspects of his life (Santoso, 2024). 
Furthermore, Akhlak is closely connected to Aqidah, serving as a reflection 
of faith through ethical conduct and spiritual development (Risma, 2022). 
 
2.1.2 ​Aqidah 
 
Aqidah is described as the core beliefs that constitute the foundation of the 
faith of a Muslim. It contains fundamental religious principles like the faith in 
the Oneness of God (Tawheed), angels, the revelations, the prophets, the Day 
of Resurrection and the predestination of God, (Qada and Qadar). These 
principles define the perception of the world around a Muslim and his/her 
spiritual self. The concept of Aqidah is so fundamental when it comes to 
aligning the believer and Allah coupled with the establishment of a sense of 
meaning and responsibility in life (Ashimi, 2023). According to Kauser 
11 
 
(2022), a solid understanding of Aqidah will block the road to misconceptions 
and support the emotional and intellectual commitment to Islam. 
 
2.1.3 ​Fiqah  
 
Fiqh, by contrast, simply means Islamic jurisprudence, the science of 
comprehending and practicing the Islamic rulings of litigation in everyday 
life. It regulates both acts of worship (ibadah) including prayer, fasting, zakat 
and pilgrimage, and also in the dealings of a person with another person 
including marriage, business and fair governance. Fiqh is based on the Quran 
and sunnah and is read by study of those whose consensus and reasoning has 
preceded. It is an effective guide that Muslims use to carry out their religious 
duties as well as make sound ethical choices (Ashimi, 2023). Flexibility of 
Fiqh also makes this religion adaptable to the changing contexts without loss 
of core Islamic principles (Solihin, Haryadi, & Rohanda, 2024). 
 
2.1.4 ​Hadiths

2.2 ​ Overview of Game-Based Learning 
 
Game-Based Learning (GBL) is a structured educational method that 
integrates the mechanics and design of games to deliver learning content in a 
more engaging and interactive way. As explained by Nisbet J. (2023), GBL 
fosters a play-based learning environment that motivates learners through 
immersive gameplay, reinforcing educational outcomes effectively. Cascella 
M. et al. (2023) define GBL as the merging of instructional content and 
gameplay, creating meaningful experiences that educate while entertaining. 
This approach enhances learner participation and simplifies complex concepts 
through interactivity and visual elements. According to Sabirah K. et al. 
(2024), graphics in GBL play a crucial role by turning abstract ideas into 
concrete, relatable visuals, improving both understanding and engagement. 
GBL offers several advantages, including short-term rewards, point systems, 
and healthy competition, making learning enjoyable. Its flexibility allows it to 
cater to all age groups from preschoolers to university students, and can be 
delivered in individual or group formats, online or face-to-face. 
 
In contrast, gamification involves applying game-like elements (such as 
points, rules, and feedback systems) to non-game settings to enhance 
motivation and engagement (H. P. Lu & Ho, 2020). Unlike GBL, 
gamification does not rely on actual games but incorporates game mechanics 
into traditional tasks to make them more enjoyable (Zhang Qi, 2022; Studart 
N., 2022). Its widespread use in education, healthcare, and finance reflects its 
ability to satisfy human desires for recognition, achievement, and competition 
(Gachkova et al., 2020; Kamboj et al., 2020). 
 
While GBL and gamification are distinct, they are complementary. In the 
F.I.K.H. application, both approaches are synergized effectively. GBL is the 
core instructional technique, represented through fully developed games like 
Laluan Hijrah, Kotak Fathonah, and Padanan Fikrah. Meanwhile, 
gamification elements strictly act as supporting game elements such as 
leaderboards, badges, score systems, progress tracking, and streak bars. It 
serves as motivational tools without altering core content. These layers 
13 
 
enhance user commitment and learning retention (Dubois A. et al., 2023; 
Farreras M. et al., 2022). As Sabirah K. et al. (2024) note, GBL enriched with 
gamified features offers a deeper and more sustained educational experience, 
aligning perfectly with F.I.K.H. 's goal of meaningful and enjoyable Islamic 
learning.

●​ Challenges 
 
Challenges in gamification create interactive experiences that test a user’s 
application of knowledge and creativity. Ehécatl Morales-Trujillo (2020) 
describes challenges or quests as tasks involving active skill use to reach 
specific outcomes. These include quizzes, puzzles, and tasks that gradually 
increase in difficulty, promoting consistent learning and skill growth. Adding 
quests fosters competence by confronting users with stimulating activities 
(Adams & Du Preez, 2022). As Gandasari & Mauritsius (2023) explain, these 
challenges work similarly to game quests by motivating users through 
rewards like points, badges, or unlockable features. 
 
 
18 
 
●​ Score 
 
Scores in gamified systems offer immediate feedback, measuring 
performance and progress. In education, they help learners track accuracy and 
mastery of topics. Hamari & Tuunanen (2021) found that score-based systems 
in mobile learning promote engagement and knowledge retention. They also 
encourage competition, self-motivation, and sustained attention by rewarding 
correct answers, task completion, or overcoming challenges. 
 
●​ Leaderboards 
 
Leaderboards visually rank users based on success in tasks or challenges. 
According to Ferro (2021), they highlight performance levels and encourage 
improvement. Cui & Yang (2023) suggest that comparing scores motivates 
users to engage consistently, as leaderboards foster friendly competition, 
improve performance, and keep learners striving for better results. 
 
●​ Badges & Titles 
 
Badges and titles act as symbolic recognition for accomplishments. Jung & 
Lee (2021) found that badges boost motivation by making success visible. 
Titles add status based on cumulative achievements. Teixeira et al. (2020) 
emphasize that such persistent rewards increase user interaction and 
commitment, especially in purpose-driven learning environments. They 
support self-paced progress and sustained engagement. 
 
●​ Streak Bar 
 
Streak bars track consistent use over time, promoting routine and 
commitment. Based on commitment bias, users are motivated not to break 
their progress streaks (Gupta & Pathak, 2022). Pereira et al. (2021) note that 
streaks improve retention and support long-term interaction by reinforcing 
daily learning habits with visual progress and occasional bonus rewards. 
 
19 
 
●​ Progress Bar 
 
Progress bars provide a visual indicator of learning advancement. They make 
goals more tangible, reducing cognitive load and motivating task persistence. 
Santos et al. (2021) report that well-designed progress indicators improve 
motivation and satisfaction, especially in self-paced settings, by offering 
feedback and reinforcing achievement through visible milestones.

2.4 ​ Justification on Game Elements and Feature Selection 
 
The purpose of the F.I.K.H. (Fun Islamic Knowledge Hub) application is to 
modernize Islamic education by making it more accessible, structured, and 
engaging for users of all ages and backgrounds. The app focuses on three 
main areas of Islamic knowledge which are Aqidah (creed), Fiqh 
(jurisprudence), and Akhlak (morality), with Hadith included as an additional 
24 
 
topic to enrich understanding. These subjects were selected because they 
provide a strong foundation in belief, practice, and character which is 
essential for any Muslim, whether young, new to the faith, or rediscovering it. 
Learning these topics helps individuals develop not only religious knowledge 
but also spiritual and ethical behavior (Ashimi, 2023; Santoso, 2024). 
 
To support this learning, F.I.K.H. uses Game-Based Learning (GBL) as the 
core strategy. GBL promotes active participation, improves retention, and 
makes complex ideas easier to grasp (Nisbet J., 2023; Sabirah K. et al., 2024). 
The app includes three game types such as board games (Laluan Hijrah), card 
games (Kotak Fathonah), and puzzle games (Padanan Fikrah). These were 
chosen because they are simple, suitable for a wide audience, and adaptable 
to both individual and classroom learning (Nisbet J., 2023). 
 
In addition to GBL, gamification is implemented strictly as game elements to 
increase motivation and engagement. Elements like scores, badges, 
leaderboards, streak bars, progress bars, and titles provide visual feedback 
and a sense of achievement without altering the educational content. These 
components help track performance, encourage consistency, and foster a 
sense of reward (Hamari & Tuunanen, 2021; Jung & Lee, 2021; Gupta & 
Pathak, 2022; Santos et al., 2021). Challenges and quizzes further enhance 
learning by prompting users to apply their knowledge in increasingly difficult 
scenarios (Morales-Trujillo, 2020; Adams & Du Preez, 2022). 
 
Compared to existing apps like Deen Quiz, Muslim Kids Educational Games, 
and Muslim Kids TV, F.I.K.H. provides a more complete and interactive 
experience. While those apps offer fun and educational content, they often 
lack features like progress tracking, personalized feedback, or gamified 
reinforcement. F.I.K.H. addresses these gaps by combining structured Islamic 
knowledge with motivational game elements, making it both effective and 
enjoyable (Cui & Yang, 2023; Teixeira et al., 2020). 
 
In conclusion, F.I.K.H. is justified in its approach because it meets today’s 
digital learning needs while staying rooted in Islamic values. Its thoughtful 
25 
 
selection of content, game design, and supporting elements ensures a 
meaningful and motivating learning journey for all.

video games, as any other kind of products, is a process that is modelled by

approach creating a game, or as large as how to react to a group of developers 
to create or to certify that a game is technologically up to date, artistically

are brainstormed and discussed. It’s also common to create a Game Design 
Document (GDD) at this stage, which outlines the game idea and checks if it 
is possible to build within the available time and resources. This early step is 
crucial, as it helps avoid confusion, disorganization, and possible risks later in 
the development. 
 
●​ Pre-production Phase 
 
The pre-production stage is the early phase in the Game Development Life 
Cycle (GDLC) where raw ideas are developed into clear, workable plans. 
According to Amin F. et al. (2023), this stage includes planning and reshaping 
the game idea, writing the Game Design Document (GDD), deciding on the 
game’s genre, story, and gameplay, and building a prototype to test the 
concept. It usually starts with brainstorming, where the team works on the 
game’s core idea, story, art style, mechanics, and who the game is for. 
Ramadiani R. et al. (2021) also highlight that this stage focuses on developing 
the game’s concept, its target users, and what makes it stand out. Nash A. 
(2022) adds that it is also important to check if the idea is practical based on 
technical needs, budget, and timeline. To test this, teams often create simple 
versions of the game, known as prototypes. Pre-production also includes 
selecting the tools and technology, creating basic visuals like concept art or 
wireframes, and preparing clear documents like the GDD and Technical 
Design Document (TDD) to help keep all team members on the same page 
before full development begins. 
 
3.1.2 ​Alpha Version 
 
●​ Production Phase 
 
During the production phase, more intricate plans will be made to make the 
game concept a reality. Designers create levels, mechanics of gameplay, 
interfaces, and stories, and make sure the game contains the engaging and 
consistent experience. As stated by Ramadiani R. et al. (2021), the main 
concern of the production stage is the establishment of the systems of the 
game, such as the mechanics of the game, its user interface, etc. The design of 
31 
 
characters and all the surrounding environments are complete, but a system 
such as AI behavior, game physics, and progression mechanics are 
documented. Joint sessions of work in the form of workshops are popular, 
during which designers, writers, technical leads participate in the idea 
improvement and solving of possible difficulties. This is an iterative stage, 
where prototypes and mock-ups can be utilized to evaluate the design 
concepts and identify ways that it will fit expectations of the players.  
 
●​ Testing Phase 
 
Testing stage is more concerned with assuring quality and compliance of the 
game with the technical requirements and user experience. The same thing is 
also mentioned by Sinnatwah, J. D. (2021) who says that another important 
thing during the testing phase is making the game user-friendly and appealing 
to the expectations of the target audience. This includes timing the interface, 
the controls and the general user experience of the game. Playtesting is done 
on a huge scale by testers to find bugs, glitches, and problems of usability. 
Paduraru C. et al. (2021) note that identifying and resolving bugs is also the 
main aim of the testing phase. This involves functional testing, performance 
testing (to make sure that it will remain stable on the different platforms) as 
well as compatibility testing. Further, it measures gameplay, in terms of 
balance, difficulty, and enjoyment. Developers get detailed feedback through 
testers and require them to go over and over correcting errors that have been 
given. This will be accomplished by re-testing the game once they have made 
some changes to it to make sure the new code did not create other bugs (Yu J. 
et al., 2023). This stage will go on until the game is steady, polished and 
prepared to launch. 
 
3.1.3 ​Beta Version 
 
●​ Beta Phase 
 
During this step, a Beta Version of the game is released to a small number of 
testers or the general populace. Galeano I. et al. (2022) argue that the beta 
phase in the game development life process is a decisive step after the alpha 
32 
 
one when the game is still tested on the real world by a certain, but not all 
external audience, receiving feedback and responding to critical problems in 
order to enhance its features and usability and the overall experience of using 
the game, preparing to its release on a larger market. Compared to previous 
tests with the inside of testing, the beta version is typically feature-complete 
but might include bugs or other gameplay changes. In the Beta Phase, the 
game is tested in a real-life environment, which gives testers insightful data 
on how the game performs, is used, and by the players. It is at this phase that 
developers determine problems that could not be identified in internal testing, 
like bugs related to specific platforms, server stability (in online games), or 
gameplay mechanics balance. The information received as responses to the 
players also assist in adjusting the difficulty, pacing, and features of the game 
in a bid to create expectations of the audience.  
 
3.1.4 ​Gold Master/Release Version 
 
●​ Release 
 
As it is stated by Amin F. et al. (2023), release stage is the last stage of game 
development, as it includes the game launch, project documentation, sharing 
of knowledge, post-mortem, and future maintenance planning, and expansion. 
Release stage of the GDLC is the period when the game is released to the 
players. Once all the development is done, such as design, coding, testing as 
well as error rectification, the game is ready to be rolled out. This part is the 
end where all the things are completed in order to ensure the game plays and 
is set out entertaining the masses. Developers make all features to be present 
and the game is tested once again with the purpose to fix all the issues. After 
completion of this, the game is then posted on app stores or the games 
websites where it is downloadable by the players to play. This step also 
includes post-release monitoring of the game to identify the issues that might 
occur and implement change, in case it is necessary.

3.2 ​ Summary of Project Methodology 
 
The Game Development Life Cycle is precisely designed to form a game 
application. It is aimed at solving problems which are not similar to those 
traditional System Development Life Cycle but which fall into the game 
industry. GDLC categorizes the development process in phases which have to 
meet functional, usable and pleasing aspects features. It is composed of 
Concept/Design, Alpha, Beta and Gold Master phases among which there are 
six (6) major milestones which are initiation, pre production, production, 
testing, beta and release. During the development of this project, only five (5) 
phases of software were performed, which were, initiation, pre production, 
production, testing and beta but no release phase because there was no 
deployment of the application on walls. 
 
The initiation stage sets up the initiation of the game idea through the 
formulation of abstractions, and the formalization of such items as the GDD. 
In the pre-production the ideas are refined, assessed on what can be done to 
implement them, and efforts are made to come up with the real prototypes. 
The production stage mechanics, systems and also game-related assets are 
developed using successive evaluations to the expected objectives. The 
testing phase is focused on finding errors, bugs, compatibility problems, 
functionality and usability testing to make the further work smooth and to 
improve the overall user experience. Finally, the beta phase ports a fully 
functional game into a real world situation though in a smaller audience and 
gathering user feedback to address performance and user interface related 
issues. Therefore, both of these stages ensure a logical building involving 
effective development strategy aimed at the realisation of the successful final 
product, an entertaining game.

4.2 ​ Application Development Process 
 
The F.I.K.H. mobile application was developed as an educational platform for 
all age groups and different backgrounds. It was built using Java in Android 
Studio for native Android development. Android Studio allowed efficient 
coding, real-time testing, and streamlined debugging throughout 
development. For the backend, the app uses Firebase to provide scalable and 
real-time services. The Firebase Realtime Database stores dynamic user data 
such as profiles, quiz scores, progress, and preferences. Real-time 
synchronization ensures data is always consistent across devices. In addition, 
Firebase Authentication manages secure login and registration through email 
and password.  
 
During the early development stages, special focus was given to UI/UX 
design. Canva and Figma were used to create wireframes, visual drafts, and 
layout prototypes. These tools supported iterative design, feedback 
incorporation, and team collaboration. Canva was used for creating colorful 
educational visuals, while Figma helped build interactive prototypes and 
responsive layouts. Resources from Flaticon such as icons, badges, and 
graphics were integrated to create a vibrant and playful interface. These 
graphic elements support navigation, reward systems, and in-game 
achievements to help boost motivation. 
 
The final design of the FIKH Mobile Application combines robust backend 
technology with thoughtful UI/UX design. Firebase ensures real-time data 
handling and secure authentication. Canva, Figma, and Flaticon contribute to 
both the aesthetic and functional aspects of the design. The result is a 
comprehensive, reliable, and enjoyable educational tool for users of all ages 
and different backgrounds.

private View createLevelView(Level level) { 
    if (userScore >= level.minScore) { 
        // Unlocked level with dynamic image loading 
        ImageView levelImage = new ImageView(this); 
        levelImage.setScaleType(ImageView.ScaleType.FIT_CENTER); 
        return levelImage; 
    } else { 
        // Locked level with dynamic text formatting 
        TextView levelText = new TextView(this); 
        String scoreRange = "(" + level.minScore + " - " + level.maxScore 
+ ")"; 
        levelText.setText(level.name + "\n" + scoreRange); 
        return levelText; 
    } 
}

4.4 ​ Conclusion 
 
This chapter presents a comprehensive overview of the development and 
evaluation of the FIKH (Fun Islamic Knowledge Hub) mobile application, 
which was designed to deliver Islamic educational content to users of all age 
groups and backgrounds through an engaging game-based learning approach. 
The application integrates three main games which are Laluan Hijrah, Kotak 
Fathonah, and Padanan Fikrah, each aligned with cognitive development 
principles and Islamic educational objectives. These games encourage moral 
reflection, strategic thinking, and memory enhancement through 
question-based progression, randomized challenges, and visual learning 
activities. In addition to these core games, the application incorporates scores, 
tiered titles, leaderboards, daily challenges, badges, streak bars, and progress 
indicators as supporting game elements to drive motivation and engagement. 
 
The technical development of the FIKH application was carried out using 
Java within Android Studio, supported by Firebase for secure authentication, 
real-time database management, and seamless data synchronization. User 
interface and experience were carefully designed using Canva and Figma, 
resulting in vibrant color schemes, smooth animations, and accessible layouts, 
all tailored for an enjoyable and intuitive learning environment. 
 
Two types of testing were conducted which are functionality testing with 
alpha testers and usability testing with beta testers. The results showed that all 
features worked as intended and that the app provides a positive, 
user-friendly, and effective learning experience. Users appreciated its intuitive 
navigation, engaging UI design, accurate Islamic content, and motivating 
game elements, confirming that FIKH is a reliable and impactful educational 
tool

CHAPTER 5 
 
 
CONCLUSION, LIMITATIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
 
 
The chapter will contain the final considerations of the research conducted on 
creation of F.I.K.H. ( Fun Islamic Knowledge Hub ) mobile application. It revisits the 
research objectives, determines how well they were met, describes the limitations 
faced during the design and execution of the project, and presents forward-looking 
recommendations on how to improve on application functionalities and accessibility, 
and learning. The general idea is to summarize the most important project findings as 
well as to outline feasible strategies of its further expansion and implementation on a 
larger scale. 
 
5.1 ​ Overall 
 
To conclude, F.I.K.H. application has shown that game-based learning can be 
an effective way of providing introductory Islamic education in the form of an 
interactive, modern interactive, and student-friendly. By applying the 
well-chosen game formats and gamification elements, the app was planned to 
help the targeted audience concentrate on three basic pillars of the Islamic 
knowledge that are Aqidah (faith), Fiqh (jurisprudence) and Akhlak (ethics 
and morality). The use of the concept of the Game Development Life Cycle 
has helped guide the project and the development process in general as, 
throughout all these stages, the project was both systematic and results driven, 
allowing to create a functional learning application, specifically developed to 
meet the needs of targeted users, which is all age groups. The fact that the 
application incorporated various types of games that include puzzle, board, 
and card games enabled it to deliver Islamic content in an interesting and 
pedagogical manner. Every game fit into particular learning outcomes in 
terms of Aqidah, Fiqh and Akhlak so that learners not only engaged with the 
contents but also the learners were challenged to think about the meaning and 
relevance of the content in everyday situations. The gamification elements, 
93 
 
such as badges, streak bars, progress indicators, leaderboards, and scoring 
concepts, were designed on purpose, with an approach to increase motivation, 
encourage long-lasting play, and support the achievement of some of the main 
learning goals. The application was positively accepted during usability 
testing and conducted on both young and older users who showed much 
greater interests and appreciation as in the traditional text-based layouts. 
These data imply that F.I.K.H. application has fulfilled its goal of making 
Islamic learning more accessible, relevant, and more fun to a more youthful 
and technology-savvy audience. 
 
5.2 ​ Research Objectives Achievement 
 
To make sure the direction of system development conforms with the original 
purpose, the accomplishment of each research objective is also outlined in 
this chapter.  The success of the F.I.K.H. application can be measured by 
revisiting the original research objectives. This will assist in keeping the most 
vital parts in focus after acknowledging and contemplating the aspects that 
were never part of the original scheme. 
 
5.2.1​ Objective 1 
 
The first objective aimed to identify the most suitable types of games and 
game elements to be used in the context of game-based Islamic learning. This 
has been achieved by conducting an extensive review of the literature and 
comparative study with those of similar Islamic learning applications already 
in the market. The study facilitated the choice of puzzle, board, and card 
games as the most effective types of the targeted age group as each of them 
provides a specific cognitive and interactive experience of divulging religious 
knowledge. These game types were attributed to the titles under Aqidah, Fiqh 
and Akhlak based on which the educational content may be presented in a 
structured order in a playful manner. 
 
 
94 
 
5.2.2​ Objective 2 
 
The second objective focused on the development of a fully functioning 
mobile learning platform designed to make the study of Islamic teachings 
more interactive and appealing. The GDLC structure informed the app 
development process and the development tools used included Android 
Studio, Firebase to manage backend information, and the UI designing tools 
Figma, and Canva. The final product was a concise prototype which covered 
game play modules, scoring systems, game progress and easy to navigate 
interfaces. All these elements have been meticulously designed with an aim 
of being in line with the values and teaching developed on the basis of 
Islamic education, so that the spiritual and moral elements of the contents 
were not lost even in the digital, gamified environment. 
 
5.2.3​ Objective 3 
 
The third objective centered on evaluating the usability and effectiveness of 
the application. Through internal functionality tests, it was affirmed that there 
was smooth functioning of all parts of the application without any significant 
technical problems. Usability testing included actual users of the target 
demographic who used the application and responded to it in structured 
forms. There were no actual pre- and post-tests, but the feedback collected 
indicated that the application had provided a dramatic increase in motivation 
and curiosity with the subject content of the learners. As the observations 
conducted during testing showed, the games with the elements of 
gamification positively affected the engagement of the users in Islamic 
teachings and thus served the purpose of the third objective. 
 
5.3 ​ Limitations 
 
The difference between a good and bad application is the ability to recognize 
and address its limitations to keep meeting user needs. Although the F.I.K.H. 
application was successful overall, several limitations were identified during 
95 
 
its development. One major issue is the need for a stable internet connection. 
Since the app uses cloud storage and real-time data sync, it cannot function 
properly without internet access. This becomes a problem for users in rural 
areas or places with poor connectivity, limiting the app’s accessibility and 
usability. The lack of offline gameplay is inconvenient for these users. 
 
Another limitation is related to the technology used. The app was built using 
Java and XML in Android Studio, which restricts the quality of animations 
and modern user interface design. Newer frameworks like Flutter or React 
Native offer more dynamic and attractive designs, which are important for 
today’s users, especially younger ones who expect high visual and interactive 
standards in learning apps. 
 
Lastly, the F.I.K.H. application is only available on Android devices. Users 
with iPhones or iPads cannot access the app, which reduces its reach and 
inclusiveness. Expanding to iOS would allow more users to benefit from the 
app. While these limitations do not take away from the app’s value, they are 
important to consider for future improvements. 
 
5.4 ​ Recommendations for Future Work 
 
In order to consider what the current application lacks, it is necessary to 
provide recommendations to facilitate the work conducted in the future and 
enhance the functionality of F.I.K.H. application. Such improvements will be 
geared towards the remedying of the existing limitations besides ensuring 
further harmonization of the application with users whom it serves. 
 
One of the key improvements is to enable offline functionality. As the current 
app relies on a stable internet connection due to its use of real-time database 
features, users in areas with poor connectivity may struggle to access learning 
content. Supporting offline mode by allowing storage of core lessons and 
game elements locally will let users continue their learning without 
interruptions. Synchronization can occur once the device reconnects to the 
internet. Additionally, migrating the development from Java and XML to 
96 
 
modern cross-platform frameworks like Flutter or React Native would 
enhance the app’s appearance, responsiveness, and animations, while also 
supporting easier updates and maintenance. This transition would also enable 
F.I.K.H. to become available on iOS devices, thus reaching a broader 
audience and increasing inclusivity. 
 
Another enhancement is the addition of multiplayer functionality, where users 
can compete or collaborate in learning games. Social features such as adding 
friends and enabling chat functions would build a sense of community, 
making the learning journey more interactive and engaging. These features 
would especially benefit learners who thrive in cooperative or competitive 
environments. 
 
Moreover, the application can be improved by creating specific learning 
modules tailored to different user backgrounds, such as younger Muslims, 
older Muslims, new converts/muallaf, and non-Muslims. Each category can 
have a unique theme and lesson approach suited to their level of 
understanding and learning style. Additionally, a Quran recitation and 
translation module should be added to support users in reading and 
understanding the Quran easily. This feature would make F.I.K.H. a more 
comprehensive Islamic learning tool that supports spiritual growth alongside 
educational development. 
 
In conclusion, these future enhancements aim to position F.I.K.H. as a more 
inclusive, flexible, and engaging platform. By addressing technical 
limitations, expanding accessibility, personalizing learning content, and 
fostering a connected user community, the application has the potential to 
significantly enrich the way Islamic knowledge is delivered and experienced 
across diverse user groups

5.5 ​ Final Remarks 
 
The results of this project clearly highlight the potential and importance of 
applying game-based learning (GBL) in Islamic education. The F.I.K.H. (Fun 
Islamic Knowledge Hub) application successfully demonstrates how core 
Islamic subjects like Aqidah, Fiqh, and Akhlak, can be taught in a way that is 
not only informative but also highly engaging. By combining authentic 
Islamic knowledge with modern learning techniques and interactive 
gameplay, F.I.K.H. makes religious education more accessible, enjoyable, and 
meaningful for learners of all ages and backgrounds. 
 
From its structured flashcards and integrated Hadith content to its diverse 
game types and gamification features such as leaderboards, streak bars, and 
badges, F.I.K.H. creates a complete learning ecosystem that promotes both 
motivation and knowledge retention. It addresses the challenges faced by 
traditional learning environments, especially for muallaf, youth, and those 
hesitant to seek help in person, by offering a safe and learner-friendly digital 
space. 
 
Despite some current limitations such as the need for internet connectivity, 
limited graphics capability due to the current tech stack, and Android-only 
support, F.I.K.H. has proven to be a promising and scalable educational tool. 
With further enhancements like offline access, cross-platform availability, 
richer user experiences, and personalized learning paths for different user 
groups, F.I.K.H. can evolve into an even more inclusive and impactful 
application. 
 
In conclusion, F.I.K.H. stands as a major step forward in the evolution of 
Islamic education through technology. It preserves the integrity of religious 
teachings while embracing innovation, transforming how Islamic knowledge 
is taught and experienced. It is not just a learning tool, it is a platform that 
renews interest, deepens understanding, and nurtures faith in a way that is 
aligned with the needs of today’s digital learners.
