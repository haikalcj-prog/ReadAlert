ABSTRACT 
 
 
In today's digital age, reliance on traditional rote-learning methods in the KAFA (The 
Quran and Fardu Ain Class) education system has become less effective, particularly 
for students who are increasingly familiar with technology. While the KAFA 
curriculum plays a crucial role in shaping the spiritual and moral foundation of Muslim 
children in Malaysia, students preparing for the UPKK examination often struggle to 
remain engaged with conventional learning approaches. To address this challenge, the 
KAFASmart project was developed to integrate gamification into a mobile learning 
application tailored specifically for KAFA students. This study aimed to identify 
effective gamification elements that enhance student engagement, develop a KAFA 
focused educational app incorporating those elements, and evaluate its usability among 
students. The project followed a Hybrid Waterfall-Agile development methodology 
encompassing requirement analysis, system design, implementation, and testing. Key 
gamification features such as badges, leaderboards, challenges, avatars, timers, points, 
and progress tracking were implemented to foster sustained interest. The resulting 
application showed significant improvements in student interaction, motivation, and 
understanding, particularly in subjects like Sirah and Adab. User feedback confirmed 
that the gamified environment made learning more enjoyable and encouraged 
independent study. In conclusion, KAFASmart effectively bridges the gap between 
traditional religious instruction and the digital learning habits of modern students. 
Future work may involve collaboration with JAKIM to align the application more 
closely with national KAFA standards and support wider implementation.

INTRODUCTION 
 
 
This chapter provided an overview of the project KAFASmart: Integrating 
Gamification in KAFA Education. It went through the background of the study, which

1.1  Background of Study 
 
The Quran and Fardu Ain Class (KAFA) is a program based on the concept of 
strengthening the foundations of Islamic education for children aged from 
seven (7) to 12, which had been implemented since 1990 (Mohd Rum et al., 
2022). This program utilizes a curriculum, textbooks, the Kelas al-Quran dan 
Fardu Ain Assessment (UPKK) examination, and follows the teaching and 
learning schedule (PdP) set by the Department of Islamic Development 
Malaysia (JAKIM) (Musaddin & Zulkifli, 2023). According to Wan Daud et 
al. (2021), this program aimes to produce successful students with the influence 
of Quranic and as-Sunnah teachings in their faith and wordship of Allah. There 
are nine (9) subjects in the KAFA program, such as al-Quran, Akidah (Creed), 
Ibadah (Worship), Sirah (Islamic History), Adab (Manners), Jawi and Khat 
(Jawi Script and Arabic Calligraphy), Bahasa Arab (Arabic Language), Amali 
Solat (Practical Prayer) and Undestanding the Islamic Way of Life (PCHI). 
 
In recent years, there has been significant interest in integrating gamification 
through mobile applications into educational tools to increase engagement 
among students. Gamification was the integration of game elements into non-
2 
 
game contexts (Moldez et al., 2024). This means that conventional study 
practices were transformed into gamified experiences by incorporating 
elements such as badges, leaderboards, and interactive challenges. These 
elements make activities more enjoyable and interactive, encouraging users to 
participate actively, achieve goals, and maintain their focus. The main goal of 
gamification was to enhance learning outcomes by providing immediate 
feedback, encouraging progress tracking, and promoting a sense of 
achievement (Smiderle et al., 2020). Hence, integrating gamification with 
learning is increasingly encouraged in the 21st century educational landscape, 
as it fosters creativity and innovation among students. 
 
1.2  Problem Statement 
 
In recent times, students have spent more time using gadgets, which 
significantly has changed the way they learned (Putri & Arham, 2024). 
Smartphones, tablets, and computers have become integral parts of students' 
daily lives, reshaping how they studied and prepared for their examinations. 
Conventional learning methods no longer capture their attention or motivate 
them effectively, as research by Aprianti et al. (2022) shows that students are 
more engaged when exposed to digital content that matches their interests and 
habits. This was particularly crucial for students preparing for examinations 
like the UPKK, where standard study materials do not fully engage them or 
help them retain information (Elman et al., 2023). Digital learning tools 
provide an effective alternative to enhance students’ focus, motivation, and 
comprehension. According to a study by Balalle (2024), students show greater 
interest and motivation when engaging with dynamic, technology-driven 
resources that align with their preferences and learning styles. These tools not 
only made learning more appealing but also create an environment that 
supports better retention and understanding of the material. Therefore, to help 
students succeed in the UPKK examination, they need better access to 
materials that made it easier to understand and master topics in a way that suits 
3 
 
their natural use of technology. This approach helped improve their 
performance and better prepares them for the UPKK examination. 
 
In reality, the KAFA program still relies on outdated conventional learning 
methods, primarily using textbooks and notes in a rote learning format. 
According to Ikbal (2023), this method prioritizes the centralized delivery of 
information from teacher to student, where teachers assume an authoritative 
role as the primary source of knowledge, and students act as passive recipients 
with limited opportunities for interaction or in-depth exploration. In most 
standard KAFA classes, this passive learning remains dominant, with an 
emphasis on memorization and repetition, using blackboards and chalk as the 
main teaching tools (Maziz et al., 2021). For instance, a KAFA teacher 
typically enters the classroom, wrote notes on the board, and students copy 
them into their notebooks, later being asked to memorize the content to answer 
questions provided by the teacher. This approach makes the teacher’s notes and 
textbooks the primary sources of learning, limiting students’ chances to gain 
additional information through more engaging methods. Even though this 
learning method was effective for previous generations, it has become less 
suitable for today's students, who are more accustomed to dynamic screens, 
animated visuals, and instant feedback (Houda Fansa, 2024). Moreover, 
students with access to smart devices may seek extra information at home 
through platforms like TikTok, but such sources are often disorganized, 
unreliable, and limited in scope (Joana & Arco, 2023).  
 
As a result of relying on outdated conventional learning methods, students 
struggle to maintain their interest and focus in learning. The absence of 
engaging and dynamic learning materials often makes the process feel static 
and monotonous rather than productive and enjoyable. This aligns with the 
findings of Pratiwi et al. (2022), who state that lessons relying heavily on 
textbooks as the primary reference create a dull environment, leading to 
boredom among students. Similarly, Othman et al. (2021) note that 
conventional teaching approaches have resulted in students becoming 
4 
 
disengaged and less active in KAFA classes. This lack of active engagement 
often leads to a superficial understanding of the topics taught, negatively 
affecting their performance in the UPKK examination. Consequently, students 
not only struggle with examination preparation but also experienced 
diminished motivation to learn and limited opportunities to develop essential 
higher-order thinking skills (KBAT), which are increasingly vital in education. 
Addressing this gap was imperative to fostering a more engaging and effective 
learning environment that aligns with the needs of contemporary learners. 
 
To bridge the gap between conventional learning methods and the educational 
needs of modern students, the development of a mobile application 
incorporating gamification elements was proposed. This approach integrates 
gamification features into the learning process, making it more engaging and 
relevant to modern learners (Yusof et al., 2021). According to Othman et al. 
(2021), digital learning tools that integrates gamification foster healthy 
competition in learning among students, which continuously increases their 
engagement and motivation. By encouraging active participation and 
presenting content in an engaging manner, the application has the potential to 
address the limitations of conventional learning systems (Hambali & Lubis, 
2022). It not only improves students’ comprehension of subjects in the KAFA 
program but also equips them more effectively for their UPKK examinations. 
Furthermore, the interactive and stimulating learning experience provided by 
the application promotes the development of KBAT and fosteres a sustained 
interest in lifelong learning, ensuring students are well-prepared for future 
educational challenges.

1.3  Project Objectives 
 
The objectives of this project are as follows: 
 
• to identify gamification elements that enhance engagement and motivation. 
• to develop a mobile application that incorporates gamification features to 
support an engaging KAFA learning experience. 
• to test the usability of the gamified application among KAFA students 
using survey. 
 
1.4  Project Scopes 
 
This project was designed specifically for primary school students aged seven 
(7) to 12, particularly those enrolled in the KAFA program. The application 
was made available in both Malay and Jawi script to align with the KAFA 
curriculum requirements. The main goal of this project was to offer a fun and 
engaging learning experience that supports students in KAFA education. By 
incorporating various gamification elements, the application aims to increase 
student motivation and make learning more enjoyable. The mobile app was 
developed for the Android operating system, allowing students to access the 
content anytime and anywhere. The content focuses on several KAFA subjects, 
which serve as the foundation for UPKK examination preparation. This project 
seeks to provide a comprehensive, mobile-based solution that meets the 
learning needs of primary school students while ensuring they are well-
prepared for the UPKK examination. 
 
1.5  Significance of Research 
 
The significance of this project lied in its potential to transform the way 
students in the KAFA program approached their studies, particularly in 
preparation for the UPKK examination. By integrating engaging and 
motivating features, the application encouraged students to actively participate 
6 
 
in their learning, enhanced their retention and understanding of key concepts. 
This tool provided a structured and accessible platform where students could 
easily access study materials and track their progress, making their learning 
experience more efficient and enjoyable. Moreover, by supporting regular 
revision through a variety of educational activities, the application helped 
students manage their time effectively and reinforced their learning in a way 
that was both fun and relevant to their needs. In addition, the mobile format of 
the application allowed for flexibility in when and where students could study, 
further promoting self-directed learning. Ultimately, this project aimed to 
foster a deeper connection to the KAFA curriculum, improve examination 
preparedness, and inspire a lasting interest in education, thereby contributing 
to the development of students' academic skills and lifelong learning habits. 
 
1.6  Conclusion 
 
In conclusion, the KAFASmart project aims to address the limitations of 
conventional learning methods in the KAFA program by leveraging the 
potential of gamification in a mobile application. This innovative approach 
caters to the evolving learning preferences of primary school students, ensuring 
a more engaging, motivating, and effective learning experience. By focusing 
on the specific needs of KAFA students, the application provides a structured, 
accessible, and enjoyable platform that aligns with the curriculum and 
enhances exam preparation for the UPKK assessment. Through this project, 
students are empowered to take control of their learning, develop a deeper 
understanding of key subjects, and cultivate essential skills such as higher-
order thinking and self-directed learning. Ultimately, KAFASmart has the 
potential to transform Islamic education for young learners, bridging the gap 
between traditional teaching methods and the demands of modern education, 
while fostering a lifelong passion for knowledge.

2.1 Overview of KAFA Syllabus Subject 
 
As discussed in the previous chapter, the domain of this project is related to 
KAFA education. KAFA is an initiative by JAKIM to address Quranic 
illiteracy and the lack of religious practice among Muslim children and 
teenagers (Hamzah et al., 2024). In the early 1990s, KAFA classes were 
conducted for students from Year One (1) to Year Six (6), using the curriculum, 
textbooks, examinations, and syllabus set by JAKIM (Musaddin & Zulkifli, 
2023). KAFA's recognition was assessed through the UPKK examinations, 
which are carried out for Year Five (5) KAFA students, covering nine (9) 
subjects (Affan, 2024). The UPKK exam was divided into two categories: 
written and practical tests. A total of six (6) KAFA subjects are tested in 
writing, namely Aqidah (Creed), Ibadah (Worship), Sirah (Islamic History), 
Adab (Manners), Jawi and Khat (Jawi Script and Calligraphy), as well as 
Bahasa Arab (Arabic Language). 
 
2.1.3 Sirah (Islamic History) 
 
Sirah, a compulsory subject for UPKK students, encompasses the profound 
journey of Prophet Muhammad SAW, covering his life from birth to his 
passing and the significant events in between. This subject also delves into the 
development of Islamic laws, Muamalat, and historic Islamic wars, providing 
students with insights into the evolution of Islam (JAKIM, 2020). As Bhari et 
al. (2021) highlight, the study of this subject aims to familiarize students with 
pivotal events related to the Prophet, his family, companions, and the broader 
growth of Islam, offering valuable lessons to guide their lives in alignment with 
Islamic principles. However, mastering this subject presents challenges, as 
students are required to memorize an extensive array of facts such as dates, 
participants, locations, and key details of events like the Battle of Badr. 
According to Mohd Rum et al. (2022), 63.3% of 30 KAFA students struggle 
with memorization due to the subject’s demanding attention requirements. This 
difficulty, compounded by the need to focus on other subjects simultaneously, 
10 
 
often hinders students' ability to retain information and understand the material, 
making it challenging for them to perform well in the UPKK examinations. 
 
2.1.4 Adab (Manners) 
 
Manners refer to a person's behaviour or habits (Ruzairi & Jasmi, 2022), 
encompassing goodness, unity, morality, politeness, and humanity. 
Recognizing this, JAKIM includes Adab as part of the KAFA syllabus to 
nurture students to obey Allah’s commands and prohibitions (Hamzah et al., 
2024) and to develop pure and upright personalities (Samurah & Che Soh, 
2022). These subject covers various aspects, including manners toward Allah, 
parents, teachers, others, as well as interactions with places, objects, and 
matters concerning the afterlife. Although this subject is often considered easy 
due to its relation to everyday life, students frequently overlook its deeper 
meanings and wisdom. Examination questions typically require critical 
thinking, an understanding of principles, and the ability to apply them to 
practical scenarios rather than relying solely on memorization. However, 
textbook-based lessons without engaging aids reduce students' interest and 
effort in studying this subject, making it challenging for many students to 
achieve high grades in the UPKK examination. 
 
2.2 Overview of Gamification 
 
2.2.1 History and Definition of Gamification  
 
Gamification is a relatively recent yet increasingly popular approach that 
applies knowledge from Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), psychology, and 
digital game development to stimulate human involvement and motivation 
(Othman et al., 2021). Although the concept of gamification had existed in 
various forms since ancient times, it evolved significantly over the years. 
According to Sharma et al. (2023), the term “gamification” was coined in 2002 
by Nick Pelling, a British software engineer. Pelling initially applied the 
11 
 
concept while developing computer games and designing user interfaces. 
Around the same time, Benthem proposed that any logical task could be 
gamified, adding further theoretical depth to the concept. However, according 
to Ratinho & Martins (2023), the first documented use of the term appeared in 
2008 when Bret Terrill mentioned "gameification" in his blog as a strategy to 
enhance engagement. Although Terrill’s spelling did not gain widespread 
acceptance, his reference helped spark growing interest in the concept. By 
2010, gamification had gained significant attention across various fields, 
including business, education, healthcare, and crowdsourcing, marking the 
beginning of its widespread commercial application (Bakhit Al-Salami, 2022). 
 
According to Schöbel et al. (2020), the term gamification sparks ongoing 
debate, as there was no universally agreed-upon definition. Some researchers 
define gamification by highlighting its relevance in fields such as finance, 
marketing, health, education, sustainability, and productivity. Others view it as 
a powerful tool for solving problems or achieving organizational goals. In the 
context of education, gamification is commonly defined as the integration of 
game elements into non-game contexts (Christopoulos & Mystakidis, 2023). 
This involves using features or mechanics commonly found in games like 
points, levels, or rewards to make content more interesting and enjoyable, 
encouraging users to interact and achieve specific goals (Alomair & 
Hammami, 2020). These elements do not convert the content into a full-fledged 
game but instead borrowed key aspects of games to enhance the user 
experience. This approach increases motivation and engagement by creating a 
more enjoyable learning environment, making it more effective for educational 
purposes. 
  
2.2.2 Type of Gamification  
 
According to Jayalath & Esichaikul (2022), gamification is divided into two 
main types, namely structural gamification and content gamification. 
Structural gamification involves adding game elements such as points, levels, 
12 
 
leaderboards, or progress bars to an existing process or content without 
changing the core material, while content gamification involved modifying the 
content itself by incorporating elements such as storylines, challenges, or 
missions to make the learning more fun and game-like (Fatima et al., 2024). 
The structural gamification approach focuses more on how users interact with 
the content or system. For example, in learning applications, structural 
gamification provides rewards such as points or levels to students after 
completing a quiz, but the content of the quiz itself remains unchanged. The 
most appropriate type of gamification used in this project was structural 
gamification, as it maintains the integrity of the original content while 
increasing user motivation and engagement.  
 
Furthermore, in the context of gamification, understanding the type of player 
or user was important to ensure that gamification strategies meet different 
needs and motivations. In 1996, Bartle introduced four (4) main categories of 
players in the gaming world, namely Achievers, Socializers, Killers, and 
Explorers (Vergara et al., 2023). These four (4) categories can be used to design 
more effective gamification elements. Figure 2.2 illustrates the types of players 
along with brief descriptions in different quadrants.

Achievers are the most common user type, with a primary focus on recognizing 
and enhancing user accomplishments. They are motivated by success and the 
recognition of their progress. In line with the study by Christopoulos & 
Mystakidis (2023), Achievers are attracted to systems that provide rewards 
such as points, badges, or levels to reflect their achievements. In gamification, 
features such as scoreboards and progress bars are highly relevant for engaging 
Achievers (Gachkova et al., 2020), as they tend to complete all challenges to 
reach the highest status. 
 
2.2.3 Gamification Element  
 
Gamification elements, also known as game design elements, are a key 
component in creating an effective gamification experience (Schöbel et al., 
2020). The main goal of these elements is to increase motivation, either 
intrinsically or extrinsically, by ensuring they are relevant and strategically 
designed to meet the needs of the user (Manzano et al., 2021). Based on Self-
Determination Theory (SDT), intrinsic motivation can be fostered by fulfilling 
the needs of autonomy, which is the freedom to act according to one's own 
interests, competence such as confidence in one's abilities, and social 
relationships. Figure 2.3 illustrates how SDT applied to gamification elements. 
When gamification elements were carefully designed, they enhance user 
experience and make tasks more enjoyable and meaningful.

15 
 
a) Badges 
 
In the web dictionary, a badge is defined as a small item made of metal, 
plastic, or fabric, featuring a design or text, often used to represent identity, 
membership in an organization, or support for a cause. In the context of 
gamification, a badge refers to a set of digital icons awarded to users in 
recognition of specific achievements or the successful completion of tasks 
or challenges (Bakhit Al-Salami, 2022). In other words, badges align with 
the concept of points by providing instant feedback to users about their 
performance (Kamis Alzahrani & Alhalafawy, 2022). Typically, badges 
carry no narrative meaning, and collecting them was optional rather than 
mandatory. Users who earn badges felt appreciated for their efforts and 
become more motivated to collect additional badges as a sign of progress. 
 
b) Leaderboard 
 
The leaderboard is one of the most widely used elements in the 
gamification concept. It provides rankings or positions for users based on 
their performance (Hallifax et al., 2023). By displaying nicknames, 
performance scores, and ranks, users are able to compare themselves with 
others. According to Bakhit Al-Salami (2022), leaderboards contribute to 
students’ motivation by instilling a sense of healthy competition to improve 
their standings. It is clear why the leaderboard is an element that is widely 
used in game applications because it is able to increase user engagement 
through elements of healthy competition. Furthermore, it offers users a 
clear visual of their achievements and motivates them to strive for higher 
rankings. 
 
c) Challenges 
 
Challenges are crucial elements in gamification, consisting of specific tasks 
that users are required to complete within a given time frame (Hallifax et 
16 
 
al., 2023). These challenges can be categorized as daily, weekly, or 
monthly, depending on the system’s objectives. Each challenge usually 
offers rewards such as points, badges, or advancement to a new level, as 
recognition for the user’s effort. These rewards not only provided 
additional motivation but also increased the users’ sense of 
accomplishment, thereby deepening their engagement. For example, in an 
educational application, a daily challenge might involve completing five 
quizzes in one day, while a monthly challenge could require achieving a 
target score across multiple lessons. With the implementation of structured 
challenges, the appeal of the platform increases while also promoting a 
spirit of healthy competition. 
 
d) Progress Bar 
 
A progress bar functions as a visual progress tracker that provides users 
with clear goals within the system (Hallifax et al., 2023). It allows users to 
monitor how much of a task they have completed and how much remains. 
This visibility encourages users to continue participating in the activities, 
as they can directly see their progress and feel more motivated to complete 
the task. In essence, the progress bar serves as a form of real-time feedback 
for the user (Fajri et al., 2021). 
 
e) Scores 
 
Scores represent a basic numerical reward (Hallifax et al., 2023) and are 
considered a fundamental gamification element, commonly awarded for 
completing certain activities (Kamis Alzahrani & Alhalafawy, 2022). As 
part of a reward system, scores serve to motivate users to improve their 
performance. The accumulation of scores also functions as a performance 
indicator that could be reflected on leaderboards. With this mechanism, 
users are motivated to make more effort, making the overall gamification 
experience more dynamic and competitive. 
17 
 
f) Avatar 
 
In a gamified environment, an avatar serves as a digital representation of 
the user, often depicting their persona or character (Christopoulos & 
Mystakidis, 2023). Avatars are typically presented in various forms such 
as diagrams, animations, or 3D graphics to make them more visually 
appealing. Users are usually given the freedom to select or create their own 
avatar to distinguish themselves from others, offering a unique identity in 
the game world. Moreover, avatars play an important role in shaping social 
interaction in cooperative environments, enabling users to feel more 
connected and recognized as part of the gaming community (Kamis 
Alzahrani & Alhalafawy, 2022). Through avatars, users can express unique 
traits, making them an essential element of a more personal and immersive 
gamified experience. 
 
g) Timer 
 
A timer refers to a specific time limit set for completing a task (Garcia-
Iruela & Hijon-Neira, 2020). In gamification, this is commonly represented 
by a stopwatch or countdown timer either counting up or down to measure 
the time users take to complete an activity. For example, in a quiz-based 
task, users might be given 30 seconds to select the correct answer. This 
time constraint introduces a degree of pressure (Hallifax et al., 2023), 
compelling users to think and respond more quickly.

2.4 Justification 
 
KAFASmart was a project developed to support KAFA students in learning 
and preparing for the UPKK examination. The project utilized gamification as 
its core focus to improve student engagement and learning outcomes. This 
approach aligned with the theory by Vrcelj et al. (2023), which stated that 
gamification in education could enhance engagement by transforming tasks 
into enjoyable experiences that encourage motivation and positive behavioural 
change. As outlined in Table 2.2, the selected gamification elements in this 
project included badges, points, leaderboards, progress bars, avatars, 
challenges, and timers, each chosen for its unique function and benefit. 
According to Smiderle et al. (2020), different gamification elements had 
different psychological impacts, contributing to a more effective learning 
experience. 
 
The project implemented quizzes as its primary activity format to create a fun, 
low-stress learning environment for KAFA subjects. Unlike traditional KAFA 
materials, which typically consisted of dense text and written exercises, this 
quiz-based approach offered an innovative alternative to help students revise 
and retain knowledge more effectively. 
 
One of the key elements integrated was badges, which acted as visual rewards 
to recognize user accomplishments. This element was inspired by the 
psychological finding by Kringelbach et al. (2016), which demonstrated that 
aesthetically pleasing visuals could stimulate positive emotional responses. In 
KAFASmart, badges were designed to be visually attractive to motivate 
students to collect them as a sign of achievement. This design encouraged 
continuous engagement and performance improvement among users. 
23 
 
 
Challenges were also implemented to offer structured, time-bound tasks that 
promoted responsibility and engagement. These challenges were grouped into 
missions to provide users with a more goal-oriented and motivating experience. 
By framing multiple related challenges as missions, the app encourages 
students to complete learning tasks in stages, giving them a sense of progress 
and accomplishment. This mission-based approach also helps to maintain user 
focus, increase task completion rates, and strengthen the connection between 
learners and their overall learning objectives. 
 
Apart from that, the progress bar element served as an important visual tool to 
track task completion. It offered users direct feedback about their learning 
progress, aligning with the findings of (Fajri et al., 2021). By seeing their 
progress visually, students experienced a sense of accomplishment through 
small wins, which helped sustain motivation and momentum throughout their 
learning journey. 
 
Another crucial gamification feature was the leaderboard, which allowed 
students to compare their performance with peers. This element introduced 
healthy competition, an aspect that Kapur (2020) argued can enhance 
creativity, learning skills, and motivation. By tracking their rankings, students 
were inspired to improve their scores and engage more deeply with the content. 
 
Scores were used as performance indicators and were awarded based on the 
difficulty of quiz questions. This system encouraged students to tackle more 
challenging questions and served as a motivating factor to improve. The 
accumulated points were then reflected in the leaderboard, reinforcing the 
competitive spirit in a positive, motivating way. 
 
The timer element was added to limit the time given for answering each quiz. 
While it introduced mild pressure, it trained students to think quickly and 
24 
 
maintain focus. The time constraint also helped reduce distractions and kept 
students engaged throughout the session. 
 
Finally, avatars were implemented to personalize the user experience. Since 
the target users were young learners, avatars helped create a sense of identity 
within the app. This element allowed students to select characters they liked, 
enhancing emotional involvement and enjoyment. For many users, especially 
children, avatars made the experience feel more relatable and interactive.

The Leaderboard Module in the KAFASmart application is designed to 
promote motivation and healthy competition among users by ranking them 
based on their scores. It offers two modes of viewing: Negeri (State-based) 
and Kebangsaan (National), allowing users to compare their performance

Figure 4.30: Snippet Code Fetch Information for Leaderboard

When the LeaderboardScreen is initialized, it calls the 
fetchUserAndLeaderboard() method. This method begins by retrieving the 
current user’s data from the Firestore students’ collection using the user's 
unique ID. The system stores this user information, particularly the state 
attribute, which is later used for filtering when the Negeri tab is selected. 
 
Next, the application retrieves all user documents from the same student’s 
collection. For each student document, it also checks whether the user has 
earned the special status of Ilmuwan KAFASmart by querying the 
status_collections subcollection. The result of this check (true or false) is 
then added into the user’s data map. 
 
Once all users' data and their Ilmuwan status are fetched, the app applies a 
filtering process. If the Negeri tab is selected, only students from the same 
state as the current user are included in the list. If the Kebangsaan tab is 
selected, the full list of students is retained. Regardless of the selected tab, 
the app then sorts the user list in descending order based on the score field, 
ensuring that the highest scorers appear at the top of the leaderboard. 
 
The sorted and filtered list is stored in the leaderboardData state and used 
to display the leaderboard on the screen. The top three students are 
showcased in a podium-style layout in the center of the page, with medals 
and colored platforms to signify 1st, 2nd, and 3rd places. Avatars, 
usernames, states. scores, and a label for "Ilmuwan KAFASmart" are all 
displayed prominently to enhance recognition. 
 
Below the podium, the remaining users are displayed in a scrollable list 
using ListView.builder. Each entry shows the student’s rank, avatar, name, 
score, and state if the Kebangsaan mode is active. Additionally, the current 
logged-in user’s row is visually highlighted to help them easily identify 
their own ranking.

gamification goals of the KAFASmart app by fostering achievement-

e) Score Module 
 
The quiz module score calculation logic is engineered the way it rewards 
the student to the accuracy as well as speed of their answers proving an 
incentive to interact with the content via gamification. The process used is 
triggered by the _answerQuestion() method when a student clicks on an 
answer. In the event that the answer is right the student will be given 5 
points. Moreover, on the event of the answer being provided within the first 
10 seconds of the 60 seconds timer, the student will be awarded 2 additional 
points and the overall point count will increase to 7 points. In case the 
answer is not correct, however, the student is punished by removing of 1 
point. The correct, wrong and skipped answers are also monitored in this 
system as the quiz is going on. The chosen correctness of the given answer, 
the time of answering, and the rest of the data are stored in a _ userAnswers 
list and analyzed later to review the results. After it calculates the score on 
the current question and writes it to the table, the system enters the next 
question after some pause by default. This type of scoring motivates

CHAPTER 5 
 
CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
 
 
This chapter presents a summary of the project and discusses how the final product 
addresses the objectives outlined in previous chapter. It also highlights the limitations 
encountered during development and testing, and provides recommendations for 
future improvements. The purpose of this chapter is to reflect on the contributions of 
this study in enhancing engagement and motivation among KAFA students through 
the use of gamification in mobile learning. 
 
5.1 Overall 
 
KAFASmart mobile application was designed, developed, and evaluated 
successfully according to all the intended research objectives. The app allows 
KAFA students to take part in quizzes and play interactive learning material 
with the help of gamification strategies that supposedly make the learning 
routine more fun and efficient. The main topics were directed to the Malaysian 
KAFA syllabus, especially the aspects of religious and ethical values worthy 
of students meeting requirements between seven (7) and 12 years old. 
 
The development was based on four major phases of the methodology that are 
planning, designing, implementation, and testing. During the planning stage, 
the project scope was defined and the associated aspects of gamification were 
determined. In the design phase: wireframes, user interface prototypes and data 
models were developed. The implementation stage concerned the connection 
of Firebase backend services to the Flutter-based front-end development to 
guarantee the real-time progression of the software and the child-friendly 
interface. The testing-stage tested usability and efficiency of the application by 
surveying and feedback by students. 
104 
 
5.2 Objective Achievement 
 
The objectives of the study were clearly defined to guide the design and 
development of the KAFASmart application. Each of the objectives was 
achieved as follows: 
 
5.2.1 First Objective 
 
The initial objective was to find the gamification features which could be used 
to improve engagement and motivation. This has been done at the planning and 
a review of literature. Several gamification components including, points, 
leaderboards, badges, progress bars, status levels, and missions were found to 
be effective with regards to enhancing learner motivations. These ideas have 
been chosen with the help of the results of the academic research and were also 
confirmed with the help of the informal feedback of the KAFA students and 
teachers at the beginning of the design process. Then, to promote constant 
involvement and to motivate positive learning behaviours, the selected 
elements were incorporated into the application. 
 
5.2.2 Second Objective 
 
The second objective was the development of a mobile app with features of 
gamification in implementing an attractive KAFA studying process. This was 
done by the effective creation of KAFASmart with Flutter and Firebase. Some 
of the main features in the application are quizzes, specific to a subject, real-
time scoring, tracking progress, badges reward system, and missions. 
Responsiveness and accessibility were considered during usability in the 
development process where testing was done to ascertain them. The interface 
was created and thought of the young learners to fit in the color schemes, 
typography, and layout according to which it could be functional and aesthetic. 
 
105 
 
5.2.3 Third Objective 
 
The third objective was to evaluate the usability of the gamified application 
among KAFA students through a user survey. This was done through usability 
test where 20 students were used within the target age group. The survey 
provided an evaluation of the aspects like ease of use, content clarity, user 
engagement and motivation effect. The feedback was overwhelmingly 
positive, and it showed that the application was intuitive, enjoyable and could 
help students with their learning. There were also complimentary suggestions 
on ways to improve the situation and these suggestions have been clearly noted 
in the subsequent sections of this chapter. 
 
5.3 Limitations 
 
Despite the successful achievement of the project objectives, several limitations 
were encountered during the development and implementation of the 
KAFASmart application. It is necessary to identify those shortcomings in order 
to develop and further execute them. 
 
Firstly, the app was created and also streamlined specifically to targeted Android 
devices. Consequently, the layout can be potentially disrupted by such changes 
in the screen resolution, the aspect ratio, occurring between different 
smartphones and thinner tablets, bringing some variances in the ways of the 
interface elements appearance. users may also experience overlapping contents 
or mispositioned buttons on some devices and this may hamper the use of the 
devices especially among the young users who depend on clear-cut pictures. 
 
Secondly, the user testing was limited to KAFA students from only Malaysian 
states, Kedah. This geographical limitation narrowed the range of user 
experiences, as students from other regions may have different learning 
environments, digital exposure levels, or others variations that could influence 
their interaction with the application. 
106 
 
 
Third, the number of respondents in the user testing sample was made up of 20 
people only. The sample size is insufficient to provide credential generalizability 
of the findings although their responses were very encouraging and could be 
taken as a sign of positive overall interaction. A larger sample with more variety 
would yield a more unbiased statistic on which usability and effectiveness can 
be judged. 
 
Fourthly, the application had most of the features that needed constant internet 
connection. The quizzes, notes, and ability to monitor the progress require data 
that is based on cloud and this poses a challenge to the students in the countryside 
or in regions with low connectivity. Such users cannot use the app regularly, 
which reduces any impact and its reach. 
 
Lastly, the graphic design of the application and the animations were not 
particularly fancy because of time and resources limitations. Although operating, 
the visuals did not include more progressive or interactive items which could 
make the learning process more engaging and enjoyable to younger audience. 
 
5.4 Recommendations and Future Work 
 
To overcome the limitations and enhance the KAFASmart application’s 
performance, the following recommendations and future directions are 
proposed: 
 
a) Make the app compatible with many devices 
 
It is advisable to increase the compatibility of the application by running 
tests and adjusting the layout of the application to more screen 
resolutions and types. Making user interface adaptive to meet the screen 
size like tablet or large smartphone screen will provide a common and 
easy-going experience to any user. 
107 
 
b) Increase testing’s area 
 
The students of several Malaysian states and different school 
environments should be covered in future assessments. This would give 
more representative feedback that would enable the development team 
to customize content and features in the KAFA education in better 
addressing a broader scope of cultural, lingual, and regional 
requirements. 
 
c) Enlarge sampling size of testing 
 
It will be important to conduct usability studies with a bigger group of 
respondents, which will provide better understandings on how students 
are going to use the application. It also can access more data and 
potentially notice a trend or problem that cannot be spotted in a smaller 
sample and make smarter design choices. 
 
d) Design an offline learning capability 
 
The implementation of offline features, like permitting the student to 
download chosen notes and quizzes to use them without the internet 
connection, will also guarantee that the app will continue to be used in 
the places with unfavourable connections. The improvement will make 
the app more accommodative and helpful to students in rural or 
underserved areas. 
 
e) Enhance visual and interactive design 
 
To make the interaction process even more effective among the 
elementary users, it is recommended that KAFASmart future versions 
would cover additional dynamic information tools like: animations of 
characters, sound effects, interactive storytelling, and animated 
108 
 
transitions. Such improvements will aid in maintaining attention and 
positive learning. 
 
f) Collab with JAKIM and official KAFA institutions 
 
Forming an official collaboration with JAKIM or any other Islamic 
education authorities may make the content fully applicable with the 
national KAFA standards. This type of cooperation would also 
contribute to popularizing the application as a tool to be used in KAFA 
schools on the territory of Malaysia, which would make it even more 
credible and more widely accepted and used. 
 
5.5 Summary 
 
In conclusion, this project successfully designed, developed, and evaluated the 
KAFASmart mobile application as a gamified learning tool for KAFA students. 
The development process followed a combination of traditional and iterative 
methodologies, where each phase including requirement analysis, system 
design, implementation, and testing was carefully planned and executed to 
achieve the project’s objectives. 
 
The application was created to offer an engaging and user-friendly learning 
environment for both students and teachers. It features educational content 
aligned with the KAFA curriculum, interactive quizzes, and various 
gamification elements such as badges, missions, and leaderboards that 
encourage motivation and participation. Based on the usability and user 
acceptance tests, the application significantly improved students’ understanding 
and satisfaction. 
 
Although the project encountered limitations related to platform availability, 
content coverage, and internet dependency, the final prototype demonstrated 
strong potential for future development. With continued refinement, 
109 
 
KAFASmart may serve as an effective digital solution to support Islamic 
education in primary settings.
